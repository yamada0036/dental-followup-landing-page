How Recall Leakage Happens in a 3-Chair Dental Practice

Most practices know which patients are overdue. The harder part is turning that list into a consistent recovery workflow.

This is an example workflow teardown, not a customer case study. It is designed to show how recall leakage can happen in a typical dental practice.
The scenario

The list exists, but the workflow is not owned.

A 3-chair dental practice has a growing list of overdue recall patients inside the practice management system. The team knows recall matters.

But the front desk is already busy with calls, check-ins, insurance questions, payments, scheduling changes, and daily patient flow. So recall follow-up happens only when someone has time.

That means recall recovery becomes inconsistent.

Where recall leaks

Six reasons recall opportunities disappear.

01

The overdue list gets too large

Patients become 30 days overdue, 60 days overdue, 90 days overdue, and 6 months overdue. The larger the list gets, the harder it is to know where to start.

02

No one clearly owns it

The dentist wants recall handled. The office manager knows it is important. The front desk tries to call when possible. But if no one owns the outcome, recall becomes a background task.

03

Patients are not prioritized

Not every overdue patient should be handled the same way. Without prioritization, the team may spend time on the wrong patients first.

04

Follow-up attempts are inconsistent

One patient gets a call. Another gets a text. Another gets nothing. Some are contacted once and then forgotten.

05

Outcomes are not tracked cleanly

Did the patient reply, decline, move, ask for a later reminder, or schedule? If outcomes are not tracked, the workflow cannot improve.

06

The practice assumes patients are not interested

Many patients are not rejecting care. They are busy, delayed, confused, or waiting for a clearer prompt.

Why this happens

Recall is easy to understand but hard to execute consistently.

The data is already there. The patient list is already there. The clinical need may already be there. But the workflow is competing with everything else happening at the front desk.

That is why recall often becomes reactive instead of proactive.

The problem is usually not knowing who is overdue. It is knowing who to prioritize, what to say, when to follow up, and who owns the result.
Better workflow

What a stronger recall recovery workflow looks like.

  1. Segment overdue patients by urgency and value.
  2. Prioritize patients based on timing, history, and likely readiness.
  3. Create patient-friendly outreach messages.
  4. Assign clear ownership.
  5. Track every outcome.
  6. Escalate high-priority patients for human follow-up.
  7. Review weekly recovery performance.
  8. Avoid overwhelming the front desk with random call lists.
Example message

Recall follow-up should be clear and respectful.

“Hi [Patient Name], this is [Practice Name]. You may be due for your next dental visit. We have a few openings coming up and would be happy to help you find a time that works.”

This message is simple. It does not pressure the patient. It gives them a clear next step.

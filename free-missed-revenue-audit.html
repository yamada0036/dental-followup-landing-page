Problem

Most practices do not lose patients because there is no demand.

They lose patients after the patient has already shown intent.

A patient calls, but nobody answers. A patient accepts the treatment plan verbally, but never schedules. A patient is overdue for recall, but nobody owns the follow-up. A dormant patient could be reactivated, but no one has time to reach out. A patient asks an insurance or payment question, and the conversation slowly goes cold.

These are not always marketing problems. They are revenue recovery workflow problems.

What we review

Four areas where patient intent quietly disappears.

The audit focuses on operational leakage, not clinical decision-making.

01

Missed calls

Was the patient called back quickly? Was the reason for the call captured? Was it a new patient, emergency, implant consult, insurance question, or reschedule request? Did anyone own the recovery step?

02

Unscheduled treatments

What happens after a patient says “I’ll think about it,” “I need to check insurance,” or “Can you send me the estimate?” Sometimes the issue is not case acceptance. It is follow-up ownership.

03

Overdue recalls

Most practices know who is overdue. The harder questions are who to prioritize, what to say, when to follow up, how many attempts are enough, and who owns the result.

04

Dormant patients

Dormant patients are not always lost forever. Some changed jobs, delayed care, forgot, moved temporarily, or never received a clear next step.

Why this matters

Growth is not only about more leads.

Many dental practices invest heavily in bringing new patients in. But before spending more on marketing, it is worth asking how many patients already raised their hand.

How many called but were not recovered? How many accepted treatment but never scheduled? How many recall patients could have been reactivated? How many opportunities disappeared because the front desk was overloaded?

Sometimes growth starts by recovering the revenue already sitting inside your workflow.
